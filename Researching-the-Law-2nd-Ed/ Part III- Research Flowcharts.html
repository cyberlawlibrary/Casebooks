PART III

Research Flowcharts

A. General Research Flowchart
B. Nonbinding Authority Research Flowchart
C. Flowcharts For Four Common Types of Research

p. 124The research flowcharts that follow are intended to help you develop a coherent research strategy. You can use them in conjunction with your pre-search filtering to plan your research path and with your post-search filtering to focus on the most relevant authorities.

A. GENERAL RESEARCH FLOWCHART

Flowchart A is a general flowchart adaptable to any type of research project.

As Chapter 4 explains, the goal of your pre-search filtering should be to use the information you already have to narrow the field of all legal information before you begin looking for authority. As you can see from Flowchart A, the more you can narrow the field of legal information at the start, the further down the process you can begin. If you know nothing about the subject, you will probably need to begin with secondary sources, either by choosing a particular secondary source or by reviewing the secondary sources retrieved from an unfiltered search. If you already have a citation to an authority on point, you can use that as a starting point.

Once you have a sense of the legal doctrine applicable to your research question, you may also have a sense of whether the issue is a common-law issue governed by case law or an issue to which statutes, regulations, or other types of authority might apply. You can use this information to determine the best starting point for researching individual types of primary authority and the appropriate criteria for post-search filtering.p. 125

B.  NONBINDING AUTHORITY RESEARCH FLOWCHART

You will not always know at the beginning of a research project whether you need to search for nonbinding authority. As you conduct your research, however, you might determine that you need to search for nonbinding authority to analyze your research question thoroughly. Flowchart B is a guide to researching nonbinding authority.

The first thing you need to determine in your pre-search filtering process for nonbinding authority is why you are searching for nonbinding authority. Nonbinding authority can serve a variety of purposes in your analysis of a research question. Here are four common reasons why you would want to research nonbinding authority:

  • When you want to buttress an analysis largely resolved by binding primary authorities.

  • When the applicable legal rules are clearly defined by binding primary authorities, but the specific factual situation has not arisen in the controlling jurisdiction. You might want to try to locate factually analogous cases from other jurisdictions.

  • When the applicable rule is unclear and you want to make an analogy to another area of law to support your analysis.

  • When the question is one of first impression in the controlling jurisdiction for which no governing rule exists. In this case, you might want to find out how other jurisdictions have addressed the issue, or if no jurisdiction has faced the question, whether any commentators have analyzed the issue.

Once you know why you need nonbinding authority, one strategy to consider is taking a second look at any nonbinding authorities you located in your initial search efforts. If the authorities you have already located prove sufficient, you should update your research to make sure everything you cite remains authoritative and, if appropriate, end your search for nonbinding authority.

On the other hand, you might review the results of your research and determine that you need to undertake a separate search for nonbinding authority. Flowchart B illustrates several research avenues for locating nonbinding authority.p. 127

C.  FLOWCHARTS FOR FOUR COMMON TYPES OF RESEARCH

The flowcharts that follow are tailored to four common types of research: state common-law research, state statutory research, federal statutory research, and federal and state procedural research. When you can pre-filter your research by one of these types of authority, these flowcharts can guide you through the research process.

  1. Can you pre-filter by jurisdiction?

    1. If no, research general secondary sources to determine possible jurisdictions (e.g., legal encyclopedias, A.L.R. Annotations, legal periodicals).

    2. If yes, go to the next question.

  2. Can you pre-filter by content (i.e., general subject area or specific issues)?

    1. If no, research jurisdiction-specific secondary sources (e.g., state encyclopedias) or general secondary sources to determine possible subject areas or issues.

    2. If yes, move to the next question.

  3. Can you pre-filter by type of authority?

    1. If no,

      1. Research jurisdiction-specific secondary sources (e.g., state encyclopedias) or general secondary sources to determine sources of primary authority.

      2. Execute an unfiltered search in an online service to search for multiple types of authority.

    2. If yes, move to the next question.

  4. Do you have any citations to relevant primary authority?

    1. If no,

      1. Research general or topic-specific secondary sources (e.g., treatises, hornbooks, A.L.R. Annotations) to locate citations to primary authority.

      2. Research individual primary authorities (e.g., cases, statutes, regulations).

      3. Execute an unfiltered search in an online service to locate primary authority.

      4. Then, filter the search results by appropriate criteria (such as level of court, date, or terms in the documents) to identify relevant primary authority. Then move to the next question.

    2. If yes, move to the next question.

  5. Will the primary authority you have lead you to other pertinent materials?

    1. If no, research individual primary authorities (e.g., cases, statutes, regulations).

    2. If yes, move to the next question.

  6. Is an independent search into individual primary authorities likely to reveal additional relevant material?

    1. If no, once you have located pertinent materials, do citators or other updating tools change or add to your research results? Be sure to update each source you cite to make sure it is current.

    2. If yes, research individual primary authorities (e.g., cases, statutes, regulations). Once you have located pertinent materials, do citators or other updating tools change or add to your research results? Be sure to update each source you cite to make sure it is current.

  1. Have you located a sufficient binding primary authority so that you need a nonbinding authority only to buttress your analysis?

    1. If yes, take the following actions:

      1. Use a citator to see if the main authorities in the controlling jurisdiction have been followed elsewhere.

      2. Consult general or topic-specific secondary sources (e.g., A.L.R. Annotations, treatises, legal periodicals) to locate leading authorities from other jurisdictions or scholarly analysis of the issue.

      3. Use a subject-specific database or pre-filter a word search by subject and type of authority to locate an authority from other jurisdictions.

      4. When done, update your research. Do not rely on secondary sources to state the law of another jurisdiction accurately. Use citators to update if appropriate.

    2. If no, move to the next question.

  2. Have you located applicable rules in the controlling jurisdiction but want to try to find factually analogous cases in other jurisdictions?

    1. If yes, take the following actions:

      1. Consult general or subject-specific secondary sources (e.g., A.L.R. Annotations, treatises, legal periodicals) to find summaries of pertinent cases

      2. Use a subject-specific research service, database, or print source to identify pertinent cases from other jurisdictions.

      3. Execute an unfiltered word search for specific factual terms.

      4. When done, update your research. Do not rely on secondary sources to state the law of another jurisdiction accurately. Use citators to update if appropriate.

    2. If no, move to the third question.

  3. Do you need to find rules in analogous areas of law because the legal rules in the controlling jurisdiction are ambiguous or nonexistent?

    1. If yes, take the following three actions:

      1. Identify analogous areas of law (subject-specific secondary sources might help with this process).

      2. Locate a primary authority in the analogous area of law within the controlling jurisdiction using secondary and primary sources as you did in your initial search for a binding primary authority.

      3. Locate a primary authority in the analogous area of law from other jurisdictions using general or subject-specific secondary sources or an unfiltered word search.

      4. When done, update your research. Do not rely on secondary sources to state the law of another jurisdiction accurately. Use citators to update if appropriate.

    2. If no, go to the fourth question.

  4. Is this a question of first impression in the controlling jurisdiction that other jurisdictions or commentators might have addressed?

    1. There is only one possible outcome, yes. If yes, take the following actions:

      1. Use general or subject-specific secondary sources (e.g., A.L.R. Annotations, treatises, legal periodicals) to identify scholarly analysis of the issue and, if they exist, legal rules in other jurisdictions.

      2. When done, update your research. Do not rely on secondary sources to state the law of another jurisdiction accurately. Use citators to update if appropriate.

  1. Can you pre-filter by jurisdiction?

    1. If yes, go to the second question.

  2. Can you pre-filter by content (e.g., general subject area or specific issues you need to research)?

    1. If yes, go to the third question.

  3. Do you have any citations to cases stating the common-law rule?

    1. If no, locate relevant case citations in a secondary source (e.g., a state encyclopedia, A.L.R. Annotations, treatises), in a digest, or with a word search pre-filtered by jurisdiction and type of authority. Then follow the same procedure as yes.

    2. If yes, look up the cases to determine relevant subject headings from the headnotes. Use the subject headings to search for additional cases in a digest or execute a word search for state cases.

Read the relevant cases, and then update your case research using a citator. Then do any or all of the following:

  • Use A.L.R. Annotations, treatises, periodical articles, or other secondary sources if you (a) have not located any case law on the topic; (b) are having difficulty analyzing the case law you have located; or (c) need nonbinding authority to support your analysis.

  • If the common-law issue is addressed by a Restatement, use the Restatement to locate nonbinding authority from other jurisdictions that follow the Restatement.

Next, read primary authorities located through secondary sources.

Finally, update primary authorities located through secondary sources.

  1. Can you pre-filter by jurisdiction?

    1. If yes, go to the second question.

  2. Can you pre-filter by content (e.g., general subject area or specific issues you need to research)?

    1. If yes, move to the third question.

  3. Do you know the statutory provision(s) applicable to the problem?

    1. If no, locate relevant statutory citations in a secondary source (e.g., a state encyclopedia, A.L.R. Annotations, treatises) or a state's annotated code. Then follow the same procedure as yes.

    2. If yes, look up the statute, and update your research for any changes. Use the annotations to locate relevant cases interpreting the statute.

Read the relevant cases, and then update your case research using a citator. Then do any or all of the following:

  • Use a statutory citatory if necessary to update your research or locate additional research references.

  • Use A.L.R. Annotations, treatises, legal periodicals, or other secondary sources if you (a) have not located any case law interpreting an ambiguous statute; (b) are having difficulty analyzing the authority you have located; or (c) need nonbinding authority to support your analysis.

  • If the statutory provision is derived from a uniform code or model act, use the annotated version of the uniform or model statute to locate a nonbinding authority from other jurisdictions that have enacted the law.

Next, read primary authorities located through secondary sources or citators. Finally, update primary authorities located through secondary sources or citators.

  1. Can you pre-filter by jurisdiction?

    1. If yes, go to the second question.

  2. Can you pre-filter by content (e.g., general subject area or specific issues you need to research)?

    1. If yes, move to the third question.

  3. Do you know the statutory provision(s) applicable to the problem?

    1. If no, then Locate relevant statutory citations in a secondary source (e.g., a state encyclopedia, A.L.R. Annotations, treatises) or a state's annotated code. Then follow the same procedure as yes.

    2. If yes, look up the statute, and update your research for any changes. Use the annotations to locate relevant cases interpreting the statute. Use a statutory citator if necessary to update your research or locate additional research references.

From this point, four paths are available.

Path 1

  1. Cases interpreting the statute.

  2. Read the cases.

  3. Update case research with a citator.

Path 2

  1. References to legislative history documents.

  2. Look up committee reports.

  3. Locate additional legislative history documents if necessary.

Path 3

  1. References to federal regulations.

  2. Look up relevant regulations in the C.F.R.

  3. Conduct an independent search for regulations.

  4. Use a citator to find cases interpreting the regulations.

Path 4

  1. References to secondary sources.

  2. Look up periodical articles or other secondary sources.

  3. Conduct an independent search for secondary authority.

The final step in all four paths is use secondary sources to assist with synthesis of authorities or to locate a nonbinding authority.

  1. Can you pre-filter by jurisdiction?

    1. If no, conduct separate research to determine which tribunal has jurisdiction.

    2. If yes, go to the second question.

  2. Do you know the specific rule(s) you need to research?

    1. If no, take one or both of the two possible actions:

      1. Use secondary sources on procedural law to determine which rule(s) apply (e.g., Moores Federal Practice or Wright and Millers Federal Practice and Procedure for federal rules; a state law treatise or deskbook for state rules).

      2. Locate the rules in an annotated code or other source. Use the index or table of contents, or execute word searches, to identify applicable rule(s).

    2. If yes, locate the applicable rule(s) in an annotated code or other source. Read the rule(s) and update your research for any changes. Research the tribunals local rules if necessary.

Next, locate research references interpreting the rule(s). Three paths are possible from this point.

  1. Use secondary sources (e.g., Moores Federal Practice, Wright and Millers Federal Practice and Procedure, a state law treatise or deskbook, legal periodicals) to locate commentary on the rule(s) and citations to cases interpreting the rule(s).

  2. Use statutory annotations to locate cases and secondary sources interpreting the rule(s).

  3. Use a digest or other case law research tools to locate summaries of cases interpreting the rule(s).

Next, read cases interpreting the rule(s). Finally, update cases interpreting the rule(s) using a citator.
